30-Day Communication Training Plan
Daily structured exercises — follow these in order. Every day has a specific skill to build. Communication is trainable — it gets better every day you practice.
WEEK 1 — DAYS 1–7: Foundation — Voice, Pace, and Clarity
Day 1Record your baseline self-introductionRecord a 2-minute video of yourself introducing yourself in English as if in a real interview. Watch it fully without stopping. Write down every filler word you used (um, uh, basically, you know, right?) and every time you rushed. This is your starting point. Do not judge — just observe. Keep this recording.
Day 2Read aloud for 30 min — focus on PACEOpen a tech article. Read it out loud at HALF your normal speed. This feels uncomfortable — that is correct. Slow pace sounds like confidence. Fast pace sounds like nervousness. Record 2 minutes and check: can someone who speaks slowly understand every word clearly? Do this every morning before study.
Day 3Eliminate filler words — one day challengeFrom yesterday's recording, identify your top 2 filler words. Today's entire challenge: do not say those words even in casual conversation. If you catch yourself saying one, pause, breathe, and rephrase. Replace "basically" with nothing — just say the thing directly. Replace "um" with a 1-second pause and a breath.
Day 4Explain one technical concept to an imaginary non-technical personPick a Salesforce concept — governor limits, for example. Explain it out loud as if talking to someone who has never coded. Use an analogy. "Governor limits are like a restaurant that only serves 100 customers per day — once full, no more orders accepted." This trains clarity of thinking, which is what interviewers actually assess when they ask technical questions.
Day 5Answer 3 interview questions — record — watch — redo each onePick 3 technical questions from this guide. Answer each one recorded. Watch immediately. For each answer note: Did I start clearly? Did I give an example? Did I have a clear ending? Redo each answer once with the improvements applied. 3 answers × 2 attempts = 6 spoken answers today.
Day 6Practice the power pauseThe power pause is 2–3 seconds of silence before you answer a question. It signals you are thinking — not that you don't know. Practice this today: ask yourself a question out loud, then deliberately pause for 3 full seconds before starting your answer. It will feel very strange. Do it 10 times. In a real interview, this pause sounds like confidence.
Day 7Full self-introduction — record 5 times with different energy levelsRecord your 2-minute self-introduction 5 times in a row. Each time, change the energy level: bored → normal → confident → enthusiastic → warm and natural. Watch all 5. Which version sounds most like someone you would want to work with? That is the energy to aim for in interviews. Hint: it is usually version 4 — enthusiastic but controlled.
WEEK 2 — DAYS 8–14: Structure — Answers That Have a Point
Day 8Learn the PREP framework for every technical answerPREP: Point → Reason → Example → Point again. For every technical question, start with the direct answer (Point), then explain why (Reason), give a real code or project example (Example), then close by restating the key point (Point again). Practice with: "What is bulkification?" Point: "Bulkification means writing Apex that handles batches of records efficiently." Reason: "Because Salesforce triggers can receive up to 200 records at once." Example: "For example, in my trigger I collect all account IDs in a Set first, query once with IN clause, then process from a Map." Point again: "The rule is: never put SOQL or DML inside a for loop."
Day 9Practice connecting your answer to YOUR projectEvery technical answer should end with: "...and I used this in my project when..." Take 5 questions from this guide. Answer each using PREP, and force yourself to end with a real project connection. If you cannot connect it to your project, it becomes an abstract fact. If you can connect it, it becomes evidence of real experience.
Day 10Practice the STAR method — tell 2 stories completelyPick 2 STAR stories from the Behavioral section. Tell each one out loud fully, from memory, without reading. Use a stopwatch. Target: 2 to 2.5 minutes each. If you go under 90 seconds — add the Situation and Task with more detail. If you go over 3 minutes — cut the Action section. The Result must always be a number or measurable outcome.
Day 11Answer "Tell me about yourself" 10 different waysSay your self-introduction 10 times today. Each time change ONE thing: what you open with, the project you emphasize, what skill you highlight, how you end it. By attempt 10, you will know your introduction so well it will always sound natural, never memorized — because you will have practiced so many versions that you truly own it.
Day 12Practice handling "I don't know" gracefully — 5 exercisesPick 5 topics you are genuinely less confident about. For each, practice the "I don't know" recovery framework: 1. Acknowledge honestly. 2. Connect to what you DO know. 3. Reason through it. 4. Express willingness to learn. Example: "I haven't implemented Salesforce CDP directly, but based on my experience with streaming data via Platform Events and customer segmentation logic in Apex, I would approach it by..." Practice this 5 times today.
Day 13Vocabulary in context — use today's 3 new words 10 times eachPick 3 words from the Vocab section. For each word, make 10 different sentences using it in a Salesforce or interview context. Say each sentence out loud. The goal: by the end of the day, these words feel natural in your speaking vocabulary, not forced. Words you only read stay passive. Words you speak 10 times become active.
Day 14Week 2 recording — compare to Day 1 baselineRecord a full 5-minute session: 2-min self-intro + 3 technical Q&A answers. Watch it back alongside your Day 1 recording. Write 5 specific improvements you can observe. Write 3 things still to work on. Seeing tangible progress is motivating — do not skip this review step.
WEEK 3 — DAYS 15–21: Depth — Sounding Like a Senior Developer
Day 15Practice "Yes, and..." instead of just "Yes"Senior developers don't just answer — they add context, tradeoffs, and alternatives. Practice upgrading short answers: Q: "Do you use with sharing in your Apex classes?" Bad: "Yes." Good: "Yes, I always declare sharing explicitly. I use 'with sharing' for service classes that run in user context, 'without sharing' for system-level batch jobs that need to access all records, and 'inherited sharing' for utility classes that should adapt to the caller's context. Being explicit makes the code's security intent clear to anyone reading it." Practice this upgrade for 5 answers today.
Day 16Practice explaining tradeoffs — "It depends, and here is why"Senior devs think in tradeoffs, not absolutes. Pick 5 questions with comparison answers (wire vs imperative, future vs queueable, Flow vs Trigger, etc). For each, practice saying "It depends on the situation, and here is how I decide..." then explain the tradeoffs. Never give a single answer when the correct answer is "it depends on X, Y, Z."
Day 17Practice whiteboarding out loud — explain a design as you drawTake a piece of paper. Draw the architecture of your Mortgage Origination Platform — objects, integrations, Platform Events, UI. As you draw, explain out loud what each component does and WHY you designed it that way. Interviewers often ask candidates to "walk me through your architecture." Practice doing this for 10 minutes without stopping. Clear verbal explanation of a diagram is a senior developer skill.
Day 18Practice conciseness — 90-second technical answersPick 5 complex questions. Answer each one in EXACTLY 90 seconds or less. Set a timer. Stop when it beeps — even mid-sentence. Then replay and see: did you cover the key points? Did you get to the example? This trains you to prioritize the most important information first — essential in real interviews where the interviewer may interrupt or redirect.
Day 19Practice English phrases for "I am confident in this"Use phrases that signal senior confidence: "In my experience..." / "What I've found in production is..." / "The approach I take is..." / "The key thing to understand here is..." / "Let me give you a concrete example from my project..." Practice starting your answers with these phrases today. Compare how your answers sound vs starting with "So basically..." or "Um, I think..."
Day 20Simulate receiving feedback and responding professionallyPractice these difficult communication scenarios: 1. Interviewer says "That answer was too vague — can you be more specific?" 2. Interviewer says "I don't think that approach would scale." 3. "Our team uses a different pattern — do you see any problems with your approach?" Practice responding calmly, not defensively: "That is a fair point — let me add more detail..." / "You raise a good concern. I should clarify..." / "I appreciate that perspective — the reason I chose this approach was..." Practice 10 minutes today.
Day 21Full mock interview — 30 minutes spokenSet a 30-minute timer. Ask yourself 8 questions from different sections. Answer each as if in a real interview: power pause before answering, PREP structure, project connection, concise ending. Record the full 30 minutes. Watch with a critical eye. You are halfway through the 30-day plan — your English and confidence should be measurably better than Week 1.
WEEK 4 — DAYS 22–30: Polish — Interview-Ready Performance
Day 22Practice active listening — repeat back before answeringIn an interview, make sure you understand the question before answering. Practice this: after hearing the question, say "Just to confirm — you are asking about [X] in the context of [Y]?" This shows listening skills, buys thinking time, and ensures you answer the actual question. Practice with 10 questions from this guide today — add this confirmation step for each one.
Day 23Practice the closing statement — how to end every interviewThe last 2 minutes of an interview are as important as the first 2. Practice this closing 5 times today: "Thank you — I have genuinely enjoyed this conversation. Learning about [specific thing they mentioned] has reinforced my excitement about this role. I do have one question: based on what you have heard today, are there any concerns about my fit that I can address? And what are the next steps in your process?" Practice until this sounds completely natural and confident, not rehearsed.
Day 24–27Daily: 1 full STAR story + 4 technical answers — new ones each dayDo not repeat the same questions. Each day pick one STAR story you have not told recently and 4 technical questions you have not practiced this week. Rotate through the full guide. By Day 27 you will have spoken aloud answers to nearly every question in this guide. That is the difference between "I know this" and "I can say this clearly under pressure."
Day 28Final weakness audit — what still sounds weak? Fix it todayReview your last 5 recordings. Identify the 3 weakest moments. Spend today's full communication practice on ONLY those 3 things. No new topics. Targeted drilling on your specific weak spots is 10x more effective than general review at this stage.
Day 29Full dress rehearsal — 60 minute mock interviewSit at your desk, dressed professionally (this is psychological — it puts your brain in interview mode). Do a full 60-minute mock interview: 2-min intro + 20 min technical + 15 min behavioral + 10 min scenario design + 5 min questions you ask them + 3 min closing. Record it. Watch it completely. Write your honest assessment.
Day 30Day 30 recording — compare to Day 1Record the same 5 minutes you recorded on Day 1: 2-min self-intro + 3 technical answers. Watch both side by side. The improvement will be visible and audible. You will hear a different person — clearer, slower, more structured, more confident. This is 30 days of daily practice made visible. You are ready.
How do you handle a question when you do not know the answer?COMM PLAN▼
  • Never stop at I don't know: Always bridge to what you DO know and how you would solve it.
  • Acknowledge the gap directly: 'I have not used that specific feature directly in production'
  • Bridge to related knowledge: 'However, based on my experience with [similar concept], I would expect it to work by [reasoning]'
  • Show your process: 'I would verify this in documentation and test in sandbox before implementing'
  • Never guess confidently: If you are unsure, say you are unsure. Wrong confident answer damages credibility more than an honest gap.
Practice phrase

"That is a good area — I have not worked with it directly in production. Based on my experience with Platform Events and async Apex, I would expect it to work like... I would verify this in the documentation before building it."

How do you structure a technical answer to sound senior — the PREP framework?COMM PLAN▼

PREP: Point → Reason → Example → Point

POINT — Lead with the answer (not a warm-up):

"Bulkification means writing Apex that processes all records in a batch with the same number of SOQL and DML operations, regardless of how many records there are."

REASON — Why does it matter:

"Salesforce can pass 200 records to a trigger at once. If you query inside a for loop, 200 records means 200 SOQL queries. The limit is 100 — you hit it on record 101."

EXAMPLE — Your real project:

"In our mortgage platform, the Loan trigger processes Account updates in bulk. I collect all Account IDs first into a Set, query once with an IN clause, build a Map, then process all records from the Map — zero additional SOQL in the loop."

POINT — Close definitively:

"The rule: no SOQL or DML inside a for loop. Ever."

Clean close matters

Senior developers close answers definitively. Do not trail off or add '...and stuff like that'. End with a clear statement: 'That is the approach I use' or 'That is the rule I follow in production'.

What are the most powerful phrases to start technical answers?COMM PLAN▼

Replace 'So basically...' and 'Um, like...' with:

  • "In my experience building mortgage systems..."
  • "What I have found in production is..."
  • "The approach I take is..."
  • "Let me give you a concrete example from my project..."
  • "The key thing to understand here is..."
  • "In the Loan Origination Platform, we solved this by..."
  • "The tradeoff I always consider is..."

For tradeoff questions — the senior structure:

  • "It depends on the context. Here is how I decide..."
  • "Both have valid use cases. For [condition A] I use X because... for [condition B] I use Y because..."
  • "In our mortgage platform, we chose X specifically because our volume was [data]..."
Avoid these phrases

"To be honest" implies you were not honest before. "Actually" sounds defensive. "Basically" sounds junior. "You know" and "right?" are filler. Replace all of them with a pause and a direct statement.

How do you handle silence and the power pause technique?COMM PLAN▼
  • The Power Pause: After a question is asked, take one deliberate breath and count 2-3 seconds before speaking. This signals confidence — not confusion.
  • Thinking out loud: 'Let me think through this the way I would approach it in a real design session...' — buys thinking time while demonstrating senior process.
  • Clarify before answering: 'Just to confirm — you are asking about [X] in the context of [Y]? Good. So...' — demonstrates active listening, buys a moment.
  • When you feel the urge to say 'um': STOP. Pause. Breathe. Continue. Silence is always better than filler words.
  • Slow down: Nervous speech is fast speech. Confident speech is deliberate. Speak at 70% of your natural pace during answers.
Record yourself

Record video of 5 answers. Watch without sound first — does your body language look confident? Then watch with sound and count every filler word. This reveals habits you cannot hear in real time.

How do you end an interview answer strongly — clean close technique?COMM PLAN▼

Most candidates trail off at the end. Senior candidates close decisively.

Weak endings:

  • "...and stuff like that, you know..." (trailing off)
  • "...I think that is the answer, I guess..." (uncertain)
  • "...yeah, so, basically that is how it works..." (filler)

Strong endings:

  • "That is the approach I use in production — and it has served us well."
  • "The result was [specific metric]. That is how I measure the impact."
  • "Does that answer your question, or would you like me to go deeper on any part?"
  • "In summary: [one sentence]. That is the decision I would make."
  • "That is my experience with this specifically in the US mortgage domain."
